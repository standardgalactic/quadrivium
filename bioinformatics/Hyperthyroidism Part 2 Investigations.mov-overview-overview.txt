The text provides an overview of laboratory investigations for diagnosing thyrotoxicosis, focusing on hormone levels, imaging techniques, and specific considerations for different conditions affecting these tests.

### Key Points:

1. **Diagnosis of Thyrotoxicosis:**
   - Diagnosed by reduced TSH with elevated T3 and T4.
   - Free T4 is preferred over total T4 initially due to potential distortions from protein abnormalities (e.g., pregnancy, medications).

2. **T3/T4 Ratio:**
   - Helps distinguish between conditions:
     - A ratio >20:1 suggests Graves' disease or toxic multinodular goiter.
     - A ratio <20:1 indicates thyroiditis, iodine exposure, or exogenous T4 intake.

3. **Laboratory Findings:**
   - Hyperglycemia due to catecholamine effects.
   - Potential hypercalcemia and increased alkaline phosphatase from TSH-induced osteoblastic stimulation.

4. **Thyroid Antibodies:**
   - Not sufficiently sensitive or specific for standalone diagnosis.

5. **Scintigraphy Techniques:**
   - Utilizes radioactive iodine (I-123, I-131) for thyroid imaging.
   - Iodine is more accurate than technetium; however, technetium allows for quicker studies.

6. **Patterns of Uptake in Scintigraphy:**
   - Increased uptake seen in Graves' disease, toxic multinodular goiter, and solitary nodules.
   - Conditions like iodine deficiency, stages of Hashimoto's disease, and increased hormone loss (e.g., nephritic syndrome) affect uptake.

7. **Factors Affecting Uptake:**
   - Iodide or iodine-containing substances can temporarily reduce uptake (Wolf-Chaikoff effect).
   - Thyroiditis impacts uptake differently during hyperthyroid and recovery phases.

8. **Ultrasonography:**
   - Identifies masses, measures size, and distinguishes between solid and cystic lesions.
   - Color flow Doppler helps differentiate types of amiodarone-induced thyrotoxicosis based on blood flow patterns.
