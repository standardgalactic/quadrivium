1. **Pathophysiology**: Acute appendicitis often results from obstruction (e.g., fecaliths or lymphoid hyperplasia) causing increased pressure, bacterial overgrowth, inflammation, and possible perforation.

2. **Diagnostic Approach**: Diagnosis involves patient history, physical examination, and sometimes imaging. Symptoms typically include right lower quadrant abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, and fever.

3. **Physical Examination**:
   - Starts with vital signs to check for systemic effects like dehydration or infection.
   - Includes inspection, auscultation, percussion, and palpation.
   - Percussion helps detect peritoneal irritation by identifying tenderness at McBurney's point.
   - Palpation begins away from maximal pain to assess for peritonitis, noting signs like rebound tenderness cautiously.

4. **Considerations**:
   - Analgesia can be given without significantly masking appendicitis symptoms.
   - Rectal or vaginal exams are less emphasized unless specific history or findings suggest their necessity.

5. **Differential Diagnosis**: Conditions such as mesenteric adenitis may mimic appendicitis, particularly in children.

6. **Management Implications**: Determining whether inflammation is localized or diffuse helps guide surgical decisions.
