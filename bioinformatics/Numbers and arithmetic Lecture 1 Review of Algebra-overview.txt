### Powers

In mathematics, when you raise a number \(a\) (called the base) to the power of another number \(n\) (called the exponent), it is denoted as \(a^n\). This means multiplying the base \(a\) by itself \(n\) times. Here’s how it works:

- **Positive Integer Exponents**: If \(n\) is a positive integer, then \(a^n = a \times a \times \cdots \times a\) (with \(n\) factors of \(a\)).
  - Example: \(2^3 = 2 \times 2 \times 2 = 8\)

- **Exponent Zero**: Any non-zero number raised to the power of zero is defined as 1.
  - Example: \(5^0 = 1\)

- **Negative Integer Exponents**: A negative exponent indicates the reciprocal of the base raised to the absolute value of the exponent.
  - Example: \(2^{-3} = \frac{1}{2^3} = \frac{1}{8}\)

### Real Numbers and Powers

When dealing with real numbers, powers can extend beyond integers:

- **Fractional Exponents**: These represent roots. For example, \(a^{1/n}\) is the nth root of \(a\).
  - Example: \(9^{1/2} = \sqrt{9} = 3\)

- **Irrational Exponents**: Powers with irrational exponents can be defined using limits or more advanced calculus concepts.
  - Example: \(e^{\pi}\) is a transcendental number.

### Special Cases

- **Square and Cube Roots**: These are special cases of fractional exponents.
  - Square root: \(a^{1/2} = \sqrt{a}\)
  - Cube root: \(a^{1/3} = \sqrt[3]{a}\)

### Properties of Exponents

Exponentiation has several important properties:

1. **Product of Powers**: \(a^m \times a^n = a^{m+n}\)
2. **Power of a Power**: \((a^m)^n = a^{m \cdot n}\)
3. **Quotient of Powers**: \(\frac{a^m}{a^n} = a^{m-n}\) (where \(a \neq 0\))
4. **Power of a Product**: \((ab)^n = a^n \times b^n\)

### Examples

- **Product of Powers**: \(3^2 \times 3^3 = 3^{2+3} = 3^5 = 243\)
