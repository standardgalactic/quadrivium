### Key Points:
- Trauma is not the sole cause of intra-abdominal hypertension or ACS; medical interventions can often prevent progression to decompressive laparotomy.
- Intra-abdominal pressure measurements are clinically useful for diagnosis and management.
- Historical context: Early studies linked IAP with physiological effects, leading to modern diagnostic and treatment standards.

### Definitions:
- **Intra-abdominal Pressure (IAP)**: Steady-state pressure within the abdominal cavity.
- **Abdominal Perfusion Pressure**: Mean arterial pressure minus IAP; assesses severity and blood flow adequacy.
- **Intra-abdominal Hypertension**: Sustained elevation of IAP above 12 mmHg.
- **Abdominal Compartment Syndrome (ACS)**: Intra-abdominal hypertension with organ dysfunction or failure.

### Classification:
- **Acute vs. Chronic**: Acute is temporary; chronic is persistent, potentially requiring decompression.
- **Primary, Secondary, Recurrent ACS**:
  - Primary: Pathology within the abdomen (e.g., pancreatitis, peritonitis).
  - Secondary: Conditions outside the abdomen affecting IAP (e.g., fluid overload, external compression).
  - Recurrent: Re-emergence after decompressive surgery.

### Causes and Anatomic Basis:
- **Primary Causes**: Intra-abdominal pathologies like bowel obstruction or hemorrhage.
- **Secondary Causes**: External factors such as fluid resuscitation or burns.
- **Anatomic Considerations**: Solid organs change slowly, while hollow viscera can rapidly increase IAP. Abdominal wall compliance limits distension.

### Pathophysiology:
- Critical illness leads to ischemia and systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS), causing capillary leak, edema, and increased IAP.
