The text discusses power series as solutions to differential equations. A power series around a point \( x = a \) is expressed as:

\[ y = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n (x-a)^n \]

where \( c_n \) are constants that change with \( n \). Differentiating term by term, the derivative of this series becomes:

\[ y' = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n c_n (x-a)^{n-1} \]

The text emphasizes that power series have an interval and radius of convergence. Within the interval of convergence, a power series represents a function \( f(x) \). The ratio test is commonly used to determine convergence, where for a series:

\[ \lim_{n \to \infty} \left| \frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n} \right| = L \]

- If \( L < 1 \), the series converges absolutely.
- If \( L > 1 \), the series diverges.
- If \( L = 1 \), further testing is needed at endpoints.

A power series defines a function continuous, differentiable, and integrable within its interval of convergence. Arithmetic operations like addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division can be performed on power series using index shifting. If all coefficients are zero in the interval of convergence, then the function equals zero.
