This summary highlights the key points from a discussion on Graves' disease, focusing on its symptoms, signs, laboratory findings, and management strategies:

1. **Symptoms and Signs**:
   - Patients typically have an enlarged thyroid gland (goiter), often vascular enough to produce an audible bruit.
   - Symptoms include sympathetic overactivity such as tachycardia, arrhythmias, tremors, anxiety, staring gaze, and lid lag due to increased metabolism.
   - Eye problems are common, caused by inflammation and immune cell infiltration in the retroorbital space. These can lead to eye protrusion and corneal changes, with severity ranging from mild (in most patients) to severe (3-5% of cases), which may necessitate glucocorticoid therapy or surgery.
   - About 10% experience eye issues without hyperthyroidism but have circulating TSH receptor antibodies.
   - Less than 5% develop pretibial myxedema, characterized by thickened, discolored skin and edema on the shins. Severe cases may require topical steroids or compressive dressings.

2. **Laboratory Findings**:
   - Elevated levels of thyroid hormones T3 and T4 with suppressed TSH.
   - Scintigraphy reveals diffuse increased uptake of tracer elements in the thyroid gland.
