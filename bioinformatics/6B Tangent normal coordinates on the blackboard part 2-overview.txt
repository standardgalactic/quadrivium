It starts by introducing key vector concepts: the unit tangent vector, the principal normal unit vector (often referred to as the binormal unit vector), and their roles in understanding acceleration.

1. **Unit Tangent Vector**: This is defined as \( \mathbf{T}(t) = \frac{\mathbf{r}'(t)}{|\mathbf{r}'(t)|} \), where \( \mathbf{r}'(t) \) is the derivative of the position vector with respect to time, representing velocity. The unit tangent vector points in the direction of motion and has a magnitude of 1.

2. **Acceleration**: To find acceleration, you take the second derivative of the position vector (\( \mathbf{r}''(t) \)). Using the product rule on the expression for velocity (\( \mathbf{r}'(t) = |\mathbf{r}'(t)|\mathbf{T}(t) \)), we find that acceleration has two components:
   - A tangential component: \( \frac{d^2s}{dt^2} \mathbf{T}(t) \), where \( s \) is the arc length.
   - A normal component: \( \kappa \left(\frac{ds}{dt}\right)^2 \mathbf{N}(t) \), where \( \kappa \) (curvature) measures how sharply a curve bends, and \( \mathbf{N}(t) \) is the principal unit normal vector.

3. **Curvature**: Curvature (\( \kappa \)) is introduced as the magnitude of the derivative of the unit tangent vector with respect to arc length, indicating how much the curve deviates from being a straight line at any point.

Overall, acceleration along a curved path can be decomposed into these tangential and normal components, offering insight into both the rate of change in speed (tangential) and the direction of motion's change (normal). This understanding is crucial for deriving equations related to curvature.
