1. **Basic Concept**: The relationship between work done by the electric field and changes in electric potential (\(V\)) is explored. Work done (\(dW\)) can be expressed as \(-Q_0 dV\) (negative charge times change in potential). Using \(dW = F \cdot ds\), where \(F\) is force, and substituting \(F = Q_0 E\), we get \(-Q_0 dV = Q_0 E \cos(\theta) ds\).

2. **Derivation**: Simplifying this relation results in \(E \cos(\theta) = -\frac{dV}{ds}\), indicating that the component of electric field along displacement is related to the rate of change of potential with distance.

3. **Uniform Electric Field Example**:
   - For a uniform electric field, this simplifies further to \(E = -\Delta V / \Delta s\).
   - Applied to parallel plates 1.2 cm apart (0.012 m), where one plate is at zero volts and halfway between the plates it's five volts: 
     \[
     E = -\frac{5.0 \, \text{V} - 0}{0.006 \, \text{m}} = -833.33 \, \text{V/m}
     \]
   - The electric field direction is from positive to negative plate.

4. **Non-Uniform Field Example**:
   - Given \( V = 2x^2 - 3y^2 \), the electric field components are found using partial derivatives:
     - \(E_x = -\frac{\partial V}{\partial x} = -4x\). At \(x = 3\, \text{m}\): \(E_x = -12 \, \text{V/m}\).
     - \(E_y = -\frac{\partial V}{\partial y} = +6y\). At \(y = 2\, \text{m}\): \(E_y = +12 \, \text{V/m}\).

Thus, the electric field at point (3 m, 2 m) in unit vector notation is \(-12 \hat{i} + 12 \hat{j} \, \text{V/m}\).
