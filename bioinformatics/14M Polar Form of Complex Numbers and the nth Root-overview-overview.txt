The text provides a step-by-step guide on converting complex numbers from rectangular (Cartesian) form to polar form, including examples and explanations.

### Process Overview:

1. **Modulus \( r \):**
   - Calculate using: 
     \[
     r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}
     \]
   - Represents the magnitude or distance of the complex number from the origin in the complex plane.

2. **Argument \( \theta \):**
   - Determine using:
     \[
     \tan(\theta) = \frac{y}{x}
     \]
   - The argument is adjusted based on which quadrant the complex number lies in, ensuring the angle reflects the correct direction from the positive real axis.

3. **Polar Form:**
   - Express as:
     \[
     z = r (\cos \theta + i \sin \theta)
     \]

### Examples:

1. **\(-6 + 6\sqrt{3}i\):**
   - Modulus \( r = 12 \).
   - Argument \( \theta = \frac{2\pi}{3} \) (second quadrant).
   - Polar Form: 
     \[
     z = 12 \left(\cos \frac{2\pi}{3} + i \sin \frac{2\pi}{3}\right)
     \]

2. **\(-4 - 4i\):**
   - Modulus \( r = 4\sqrt{2} \).
   - Argument \( \theta = \frac{5\pi}{4} \) (third quadrant).
   - Polar Form:
     \[
     z = 4\sqrt{2} \left(\cos \frac{5\pi}{4} + i \sin \frac{5\pi}{4}\right)
     \]

3. **\(3 - 3i\):**
   - Modulus \( r = 3\sqrt{2} \).
   - Argument \( \theta = \frac{7\pi}{4} \) (fourth quadrant).
   - Polar Form:
     \[
     z = 3\sqrt{2} \left(\cos \frac{7\pi}{4} + i \sin \frac{7\pi}{4}\right)
     \]

4. **\(1 - \sqrt{3}i\):**
   - Modulus \( r = 2 \).
   - Argument \( \theta = \frac{5\pi}{3} \) (fourth quadrant, similar adjustment as previous example).
