2. **Shoulder Dislocations:**
   - Commonly seen in sports or traumatic injuries, characterized by a flattened shoulder contour and restricted motion.
   - Diagnosis often involves x-rays showing the head of the humerus out of place, with specific signs like the "Mercedes-Benz sign."
   - Reduction methods include the Cocker method (using counter traction) and the Hippocratic method (levering back into place). The process should be gentle to avoid further injury.

3. **Nerve and Tendon Injuries:**
   - For nerve injuries, especially around the shoulder and hand, assessment includes checking flexor tendons of fingers using specific tests.
   - Rehabilitation post-reduction involves a sling for four weeks followed by physiotherapy.

### Pediatric Fractures (Salty Hair Fractures)
- **Occurrence**: Found in children, more common with younger ages.
- **Types**:
  - **Type 1**: Fracture parallel to the growth plate without involving the shaft or metaphysis; has a good prognosis.
  - **Type 2**: Involves a fragment through the shaft but not the metaphysis or growth plate; also generally favorable.
  - **Type 3-5**: More serious, involving fractures that extend through the metaphysis and/or growth plate, with higher risk of growth deformities. Type 4 involves the shaft as well, while type 5 is an impacted fracture.
- **Management**:
  - Undisplaced: Conservative treatment.
  - Displaced: Closed reductions are recommended; best done earlier to avoid complications after healing begins (within 5 days).

### Adult Shoulder Dislocation
- **Common Type**: Anterior dislocation often resulting from sports injuries or forceful abduction and external rotation.
- **Clinical Presentation**:
  - Flattened shoulder contour, fullness in the front, limited range of motion with pain during internal rotation and abduction.
  - Auxiliary nerve damage may occur; test for deltoid sensation.
- **Diagnosis**: X-rays show displacement where the humeral head is not articulating with the glenoid (Mercedes-Benz sign).
- **Management**:
  - Reduction in casualty using methods like Cocker or Hippocratic, emphasizing gentle traction and muscle relaxation.
  - May require muscle relaxers if necessary.
- **Rehabilitation**: Four weeks of sling support followed by physiotherapy to strengthen shoulder muscles.

### Hand Examination for Tendon Injury
- **Flexor Tendons**:
  - Testing involves isolating finger movement to identify damage to flexor digitorum superficialis (FDS) and profundus (FDP).
- **Wrist Flexors**:
  - Palmaris longus, flexor carpi radialis, and ulnaris are tested by asking the patient to point fingers upward.
- **Extensor Tendons**:
  - Tested against resistance with hand turned over on a table.
- **Thumb Extensors**:
  - Special attention required for testing due to its unique structure.

1. **Thumb Movements**:
   - Distal or interphalangeal flexion is controlled by the *flexor pollicis longus*.
   - Extension involves the *extensor pollicis longus*. Both should be tested individually.

2. **Nerve Supply to the Hand**:
   - There are three main nerves: median, ulnar, and radial.
   - The *median nerve* primarily affects the thumb and the index finger, along with half of the middle finger. A motor test checks for tension in the thenar eminence muscles.
   - The *ulnar nerve* affects the little finger, half of the ring finger, and the volar surfaces on the ulnar side of the hand. Motor function is tested through the interosseous muscles with a "wagging middle finger" sign indicating intact function.
   - The *radial nerve* does not control motor functions in the hand but provides extension for fingers and wrists. Its sensory distribution includes the dorsum between the thumb and index finger.
