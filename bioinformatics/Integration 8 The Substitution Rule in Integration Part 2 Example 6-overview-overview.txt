To find the indefinite integral of \(\cos(2x)\) with respect to \(x\) using substitution, follow these steps:

1. **Substitution**: Let \(u = 2x\). Then, differentiate to get \(du = 2 \, dx\), which rearranges to \(dx = \frac{1}{2} \, du\).

2. **Rewriting the Integral**: Substitute \(u\) and \(dx\) into the integral:
   \[
   \int \cos(2x) \, dx = \int \cos(u) \cdot \frac{1}{2} \, du
   \]

3. **Simplifying**: Factor out the constant \(\frac{1}{2}\):
   \[
   \frac{1}{2} \int \cos(u) \, du
   \]

4. **Integration**: Integrate \(\cos(u)\) to get \(\sin(u)\), resulting in:
   \[
   \frac{1}{2} \sin(u) + C
   \]

5. **Substitute Back**: Replace \(u\) with \(2x\):
   \[
   \frac{1}{2} \sin(2x) + C
   \]

Thus, the indefinite integral of \(\cos(2x)\) is \(\frac{1}{2} \sin(2x) + C\).
