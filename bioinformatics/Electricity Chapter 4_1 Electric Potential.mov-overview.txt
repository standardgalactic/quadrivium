### Electric Potential Change in a Uniform Field

1. **Electric Potential (V):** 
   - Defined as the work done by an external force to bring a charge from infinity to a point in space, per unit charge.
   - Mathematically: \( V = - \int_{\text{initial}}^{\text{final}} \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{s} \)
   - In a uniform electric field (\(\mathbf{E}\)), this simplifies to \( V = - E \cdot d \), where \(d\) is the displacement along the direction of the field.

2. **Equipotential Surfaces:**
   - A surface or line where the electric potential is constant.
   - In a uniform field, lines perpendicular to the field lines are equipotential surfaces because there's no change in potential (\( \Delta V = 0 \)) when moving along these lines.

### Path Independence

- **Conservative Force:**
  - The electrostatic force is conservative, meaning the work done by this force on a charge moving between two points is independent of the path taken.
  - This implies that the change in electric potential (\( \Delta V \)) depends only on the initial and final positions.

### Example with Uniform Electric Field

1. **From Point I to F:**
   - If moving along the direction of the field, \( \Delta V = - E \cdot d \).
   - The negative sign indicates a decrease in potential when moving in the direction of the electric field.

2. **Equipotential Surface (C to F):**
   - Moving perpendicular to the field lines results in no change in potential (\( \Delta V = 0 \)).

3. **Path Independence:**
   - Whether you move directly from I to F or via a detour through C, the net change in potential is the same.
   - This is shown by calculating \( \Delta V \) for different paths and finding them equal due to trigonometric identities (e.g., \( L = K / \cos(\theta) \)).
