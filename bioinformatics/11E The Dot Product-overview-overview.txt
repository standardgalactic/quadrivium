The provided text explains the concept of the dot product (also known as the Euclidean inner product) in linear algebra and its two main definitions:

1. **Geometric Definition**: The dot product of two non-zero vectors \(\mathbf{u}\) and \(\mathbf{v}\) is calculated using their magnitudes and the cosine of the angle between them:
   \[
   \mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = \|\mathbf{u}\| \|\mathbf{v}\| \cos(\theta)
   \]
   This results in a scalar, which is zero if either vector has zero magnitude or if the vectors are perpendicular (\(90^\circ\) apart).

2. **Algebraic Definition**: For vectors \(\mathbf{u} = (u_1, u_2, u_3)\) and \(\mathbf{v} = (v_1, v_2, v_3)\), the dot product is:
   \[
   \mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = u_1v_1 + u_2v_2 + u_3v_3
   \]
   This involves multiplying corresponding components of the vectors and summing them to yield a scalar.
