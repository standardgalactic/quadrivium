The text describes solving the indefinite integral of \( \frac{1}{\cos^2(2y)} \) with respect to \( y \). It starts by rewriting the integrand using the trigonometric identity \( \frac{1}{\cos(\theta)} = \sec(\theta) \), transforming it into \( \int \sec^2(2y) \, dy \).

To simplify this integral, a substitution is used: let \( u = 2y \), which implies \( du = 2 \, dy \) or \( dy = \frac{1}{2} \, du \). Substituting these into the integral results in:

\[
\int \sec^2(2y) \, dy = \frac{1}{2} \int \sec^2(u) \, du
\]

Recognizing that the derivative of \( \tan(\theta) \) is \( \sec^2(\theta) \), the integral becomes:

\[
\frac{1}{2} \int \sec^2(u) \, du = \frac{1}{2} \tan(u) + C
\]

Substituting back \( u = 2y \), the final solution is:

\[
\frac{1}{2} \tan(2y) + C
\]

This expression represents the indefinite integral of \( \frac{1}{\cos^2(2y)} \) with respect to \( y \).
