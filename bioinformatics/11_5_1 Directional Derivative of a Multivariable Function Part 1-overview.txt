1. **Partial Derivatives**: Initially, partial derivatives are introduced as measures of how a function changes when each variable is altered independently while keeping other variables constant. In three-dimensional space, this results in examining changes along the x, y, or z axes.

2. **Gradient**: The gradient of a function combines these partial derivatives into a vector that points in the direction of the steepest ascent. For a function \( f(x, y, z) \), the gradient is given by \( (\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}, \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}, \frac{\partial f}{\partial z}) \).

3. **Directional Derivative**: Unlike partial derivatives that measure changes along coordinate axes, directional derivatives consider changes in any specified direction. To compute a directional derivative:
   - Choose a unit vector \( \mathbf{u} = (a, b) \) indicating the desired direction.
   - If necessary, convert this to a unit vector by dividing it by its norm: \( \frac{\mathbf{v}}{\|\mathbf{v}\|} \).
   - The directional derivative in the direction of \( \mathbf{u} \) is the dot product of the gradient and \( \mathbf{u} \): \( \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{u} \).

4. **Interpretation**: The value of a directional derivative depends on the angle between the gradient vector and the direction vector:
   - It reaches its maximum when the direction aligns with the gradient.
   - It is zero when perpendicular to the gradient.
   - It is minimized (negative) when opposite to the gradient.

This concept allows for more nuanced understanding and analysis of how functions change in various directions, beyond just along standard coordinate axes.
