Checking Precalculus and Trigonometry/03. Complex Numbers.txt
1. The Mandelbrot set is a set of complex numbers that, when used as inputs in an iterative function, do not escape to infinity. This set is characterized by its boundary, which exhibits an infinite amount of self-similar detail at all scales of magnification.

2. The iterative function commonly used to define the Mandelbrot set is f(c) = c^2, where c is a complex number and f(c) is a new complex number. This function is applied iteratively, meaning the output of one step is input into the next step.

3. To determine whether a complex number c is in the Mandelbrot set, we start with an initial value (often zero) and apply the function repeatedly, checking if the magnitude (or absolute value) of the resulting sequence stays bounded. If it does, c is in the Mandelbrot set; if not, c is not in the set.

4. The computation for 1 + i (one plus the imaginary unit i) is unbounded, indicating that it is not in the Mandelbrot set. However, i (the imaginary unit itself) is in the Mandelbrot set because when we apply the iterative function i^2 = -1, and then continue the iteration, the sequence stays bounded.

5. The Mandelbrot set can be visualized by plotting all complex numbers and coloring them based on whether or not they are bounded under iteration. The result is a fractal that reveals intricate patterns when zoomed in on.

6. Fractals, like the Mandelbrot set, are geometric shapes that exhibit self-similarity across different scales and are often generated by repeating a simple process over and over in an ongoing feedback loop.

7. In the next lecture, we will explore rational functions, which are expressions of the form P(x)/Q(x), where P(x) and Q(x) are polynomials. We will discuss their properties, domains, and applications.

The Mandelbrot set is a fundamental example of a mathematical object that is best understood visually and computationally, highlighting the interplay between mathematics, art, and computer graphics.

Checking Precalculus and Trigonometry/04. Rational Functions.txt
1. Rational Functions Recap:
   - A rational function is a fraction of two polynomials, where the denominator is not zero (no undefined vertical asymptotes).
   - The horizontal asymptote occurs when the degree of the numerator and the denominator are the same or if the degree of the numerator is one more than the denominator. It represents the line that the graph approaches as x goes to infinity or negative infinity.
   - Zeros (or roots) are the x-values where the numerator equals zero, and they are excluded from the domain of the function since division by zero is undefined.
   - Vertical asymptotes occur at the zeros of the denominator (unless those zeros are also in the numerator).

2. True-False Questions:
   - A rational function can have an infinite number of vertical asymptotes: False. A rational function can only have a finite number of vertical asymptotes, dependent on the degree of the polynomial in the denominator.
   - A rational function must have at least one vertical asymptote: False. If the denominator never equals zero (for all real x), there will be no vertical asymptotes.
   - The graph of a rational function can cross a horizontal asymptote: True. The graph may cross the horizontal asymptote, but it cannot eventually cross or intersect with it as x goes to infinity or negative infinity.
   - The graph of a rational function can cross a vertical asymptote: False. The graph cannot cross a vertical asymptote because that point is not in the domain of the function due to the denominator being zero.

3. Preview of Next Topic: Inverse Functions:
   - Inverse functions undo each other, effectively taking you back to your starting point.
   - The concept of inverse functions is crucial for understanding exponential and logarithmic functions as well as trigonometric functions and their inverses.
   - Inverse functions will be a key topic in the study of calculus and are an essential part of pre-calculus preparation.

Checking Precalculus and Trigonometry/07. Exponential Functions.txt
1. **Exponential Functions**: Exponential functions can either increase or decrease. An exponential function of the form `f(x) = a^x` (where `a` is a real number and `a > 0`) is always one-to-one, meaning each output corresponds to exactly one input. For `a > 1`, the function represents exponential growth, while for `0 < a < 1`, it represents exponential decay.

2. **Comparison between Exponential and Polynomial Growth**: When comparing an exponential function with a polynomial, the exponential function will eventually outgrow the polynomial. For example, the graph of `y = x^10` (a polynomial) and `y = 2^x` (an exponential function) shows that for values of `x` larger than 59, the exponential function `2^x` becomes larger than `x^10`. This illustrates that an exponential function grows faster than any polynomial function for sufficiently large inputs.

3. **The Number E**: The number E is an irrational number that comes up frequently in mathematics, especially in calculus and its applications. It can be approximated by the sum of the infinite series `1 + 1/2 + 1/6 + 1/24 + ...` and has a value approximately equal to 2.71828. The significance of E lies in its role in various mathematical formulas, including the natural logarithm function and the area under the curve of `y = 1/x` from 1 to `e`.

4. **Infinite Series**: An infinite series is a sum of an endless sequence of numbers. In the case of E, the infinite series converges to E. Understanding infinite series is crucial in many areas of mathematics and physics.

5. **Next Topic**: The instructor hints at the next topic being logarithms, which are the inverses of exponential functions. Logarithms will allow us to solve equations where the unknown variable is in an exponent, among other applications.

In summary, exponential functions are versatile and can model growth or decay, and they ultimately outpace any polynomial function for large values of `x`. The number E is a fundamental constant that appears naturally in many mathematical formulas and theories. Understanding these concepts will be essential as you progress through calculus and beyond.

Checking Precalculus and Trigonometry/09. Properties of Logarithms.txt
1. **Exponential Function (e^x)**:
   - Graphs approach the x-axis but never touch it, approaching y = 0 as x approaches negative infinity and y approaches positive infinity.
   - Horizontal asymptote is the x-axis (y = 0).
   - Intercept at (0, 1).
   - Domain: All real numbers (-∞, ∞).
   - Range: All positive real numbers (0, ∞).

2. **Natural Logarithmic Function (ln(x))**:
   - Graphs are above the x-axis for x > 0 and below the x-axis for x < 0, with the y-axis as the vertical asymptote.
   - Horizontal asymptote is the y-axis (y = ∞).
   - Intercept at (1, 0).
   - Domain: All positive real numbers (0, ∞).
   - Range: All real numbers (-∞, ∞).

3. **Symmetry and Inverse Relationship**:
   - The exponential function e^x and the natural logarithmic function ln(x) are inverse functions, meaning their composition results in the identity function y = x.
   - For every point (y, x) on the graph of e^x, there is a corresponding point (x, y) on the graph of ln(x), and vice versa.

4. **Domain and Range Interchange for Inverse Functions**:
   - If f(x) has domain A and range B, then its inverse f^(-1)(x) will have range A and domain B.

5. **Change of Base Formula**:
   - The value of a logarithm can be changed by multiplying the exponent by the change in base divided by the original base.

6. **Logarithmic Properties**:
   - Log of a product is the sum of the logs (log(ab) = log(a) + log(b)).
   - Log of a power is the exponent times the log of the base (log(a^n) = n * log(a)).
   - Log of a quotient is the difference of the logs (log(a/b) = log(a) - log(b)).

7. **Equivalence of Expressions**:
   - The functions f(x) = log(x / 2), g(x) = log(x) - log(2), and h(x) = log(sqrt(8)) are equivalent to each other through logarithmic properties.

8. **Manipulation of Logarithmic Expressions**:
   - The expression log(sqrt(8)) is equivalent to 3 * (1/2) * log(2), which simplifies to 3 * (1/2) * log(2^(1/2)), and further simplifies to 3 * (1/2) * (1/2) * log(2), resulting in 3/4 * log(2).

In summary, we've recapped the important properties of exponential and logarithmic functions, emphasizing their interconnectedness and how to manipulate logarithmic expressions. Next time, we'll apply these concepts to solve equations involving logarithms and exponents.

   - Directrix: 2 units away from the vertex but in the opposite direction, on the line \( x = h \), so \( x = -2 \)

1. **Slope at a Point:** The slope of the tangent line to the graph of y = x^2 - 4/x - 2 at the point (2, 0) is not immediately apparent from the parabola's shape alone. However, by calculating the slope as x approaches 2, we can determine it using limits or algebraically.

2. **Algebraic Approach:** The equation y = x^2 - 4/(x - 2) can be factored and simplified to y = (x + 2)(x - 2). As x gets closer to 2, the slope of the tangent line approaches 4.

3. **Limit Notation:** The limit of the function y = x^2 - 4/(x - 2) as x approaches 2 is 4, which means the slope of the tangent line at x = 2 is 4. This is an application of the concept of a derivative in calculus, where the derivative of y = x^2 at x = 2 is 4.

4. **Point-Slope Form:** Using the point (2, 0) and the slope of 4, we can write the equation of the tangent line as y - 0 = 4(x - 2). Simplifying this gives y = 4x - 4.

5. **Graphical Representation:** By graphing both the parabola x^2 and the straight line 4x - 4 on a graphing calculator, you can observe the tangent point where the two curves have the same slope, and the line appears to touch the curve at that single point before continuing on its own path.
