1. **Parabolas** have one axis of symmetry, represented by an equation of the form `y = ax^2 + bx + c` or `x = ay^2 + by + c`. They open either upwards or downwards (or to the right or left if x is the independent variable).

2. **Hyperbolas** have two axes of symmetry, represented by an equation of the form `(ax^2 - b^2) / x^2 - (ay^2 - c^2) / y^2 = 1` or its variations. 3. **Ellipses** have two axes of symmetry, represented by an equation of the form `(x^2 / a^2) + (y^2 / b^2) = 1`. Ellipses open into a closed loop and can be horizontal or vertical depending on the relative values of `a` and `b`.

4. **Circles** have one axis of symmetry, represented by an equation of the form `(x - h)^2 + (y - k)^2 = r^2`, where `(h, k)` is the center of the circle and `r` is the radius. Circles are special cases of ellipses that are perfect loops.

5. **Graphing Conic Sections**: To graph conic sections, identify the standard form of the equation, determine the values of any given elements (center coordinates `(h, k)`, lengths for `a`, `b`, `c`, and `r`), and use this information to plot key points, calculate slopes, or draw the curves.

6. **Standard Forms**: Conic section equations are often put into standard forms that make it easier to identify the type of conic and to graph them. The standard form for each type is as follows:
   - Parabolas: `y = ax^2 + bx + c` or `x = ay^2 + by + c`
   - Hyperbolas: `(ax^2 - b^2) / x^2 - (ay^2 - c^2) / y^2 = 1`
   - Ellipses: `(x^2 / a^2) + (y^2 / b^2) = 1`
   - Circles: `(x - h)^2 + (y - k)^2 = r^2`

7. **Key Differences**: The key differences between the conic sections are determined by the coefficients and the signs in their equations, which dictate the shape and orientation of the graph. The presence of squared terms and their relative signs and coefficients are crucial for distinguishing between parabolas, hyperbolas, ellipses, and circles.

1. **Permutations vs Combinations**: Permutations refer to the different ways to arrange a set of items where order matters, while combinations refer to the different ways to choose a subset of items from a larger set without regard to order.

2. **Formulas for Permutations and Combinations**:
   - **Permutation (n choose k)**: \( P(n, k) = \frac{n!}{(n-k)!} \)
   - **Combination (n choose k)**: \( C(n, k) = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!} \) where \( n! \) denotes the factorial of n.

3. **Calculating Probabilities**: Use the multiplication principle and combinations to calculate probabilities. For example:
   - To find the probability of drawing a full house from a standard 52-card deck, you multiply the number of ways to draw three of a kind (\( C(4,3) \times 13 \)) by the number of ways to draw a pair from the remaining 49 cards (\( C(4,2) \)).

4. **Example: Probability of Drawing a Full House**:
   - \( 3 \times 13 \times 7 \times C(4,2) = 3744 \) ways to draw a full house.
   - Probability \( = \frac{3744}{2,598,960} \).

5. **Example: Probability of Drawing a Straight**:
   - Choose the lowest card in the straight (10 options for the starting card).
   - Pick 4 consecutive cards from the remaining 48 cards (4 options per pile, one for each position in the straight).
   - Total ways to draw a straight \( = 10 \times 4^5 \).
   - Probability \( = \frac{10 \times 4^5}{2,598,960} \).

6. **Key Takeaways**:
   - Understanding the difference between permutations and combinations is crucial for solving probability problems.
   - The multiplication principle allows you to break down complex probability scenarios into simpler parts that can be solved using combinations.
   - Always remember to account for all possible outcomes and use factorials appropriately when calculating permutations and combinations.
